\section{Introduction}

Robots need spatial perception that remains reliable across platforms, sensors, and operating conditions, yet achieving this reliability is difficult because both the environment and the robot itself are sources of variability.
On the environment side, lighting shifts, scenes contain dynamic objects and textureless regions, and fast motion introduces blur and occlusions~\cite{qin2018vins, mur2015orb}.
On the robot side, sensor characteristics vary across cameras and IMUs, vibration and temperature change noise statistics, and calibration parameters can drift over time~\cite{geneva2020openvins, furgale2013unified}.
As a consequence, most visual SLAM and inertial navigation systems break when they leave the regimes they were calibrated for~\cite{forster2015imu, qin2018vins}, blocking the goal of spatial perception that works for \textbf{any robot, anywhere, anytime without manual tuning}.

I argue that the limiting factor is not only models or data, but also how systems learn and calibrate uncertainty.
Classical pipelines rely on hand-tuned, fixed covariance matrices that assume stationary Gaussian noise~\cite{forster2015imu, qin2018vins, mur2015orb}.
Because these covariances do not adapt to context, they become overconfident under lighting changes, texture dropouts, or platform-dependent IMU vibration, and underconfident in benign conditions.
When two sensors are fused with miscalibrated weights, the estimator either over-trusts a degraded modality or ignores a reliable one, producing state estimates that are worse than either sensor alone.
Deployment on a new robot therefore requires human retuning; sensor fusion inherits brittle weights that degrade estimation; and downstream tasks such as planning and control receive unreliable state estimates.

Learning-based odometry has improved accuracy~\cite{teed2021droid}, but it typically outputs scale-agnostic confidence weights rather than calibrated, metric covariances, which prevents principled fusion and weakens failure detection.

My research develops \textit{learned uncertainty} as a unifying principle for building robust perception systems, not as an isolated prediction target.
Rather than treating covariance as a fixed parameter, I learn metrics-aware covariance from data and integrate it into full estimation pipelines that transfer across cameras, IMUs, platforms, and conditions.
This approach enables three coupled system capabilities: robust state estimation, principled multi-modal fusion and calibration, and efficient deployment through uncertainty-guided compute allocation.
Figure~\ref{fig:overview} illustrates how these capabilities connect across the vision and inertial sensing pipeline.

\section{Past and Current Research}
My research focuses on leveraging learned metrics-aware covariance to build robust state estimation systems, principled sensor fusion and calibration, and efficient deployment across sensors and platforms.
Vision is the primary sensing modality for spatial understanding, so the first step builds a robust visual odometry system using metrics-aware visual covariance for correspondence filtering and residual weighting.
Visual evidence degrades under occlusion and aggressive motion, so the second step develops a learning-based inertial model with corresponding uncertainty for reliable inertial perception.
With both modalities producing metrics-aware covariance, the third step enables tuning-free visual-inertial initialization and calibration through reliability-aware fusion.
After robustness and fusion are established, the fourth step uses uncertainty as a guidance signal for model acceleration and efficient edge deployment.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Metric-aware Covariance for Stereo Visual Odometry.}

We developed MAC-VO, a learning-based stereo visual odometry system~\cite{anonymous2025macvo}, that predicts metrics-aware covariance from visual features and replaces hand-tuned geometric noise models.
We train the model on a large-scale synthetic dataset~\cite{anonymous2020tartanair}, and the predicted covariance preserves metric scale and transfers to unseen real cameras.
The method predicts 2D correspondence uncertainty from appearance, context, and occlusion cues, then lifts it through stereo geometry into a full 3D covariance with inter-axis correlations.
The resulting covariance filters unreliable correspondences and weights residuals in pose optimization within a full VO pipeline.
This design yields robust, state-of-the-art visual odometry performance with strong cross-camera generalization and less per-platform retuning.
It also provides a tuning-free interface for downstream fusion because each visual constraint carries a learned reliability estimate instead of a hand-tuned weight.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Data-Driven Inertial Odometry and Uncertainty Propagation.}
Visual-only estimation is brittle under occlusion and aggressive motion, so the next step develops a learning-based inertial model with metrics-aware uncertainty.
A data-driven framework~\cite{anonymous2025airimu} learns residual IMU correction and uncertainty propagation within a differentiable preintegration pipeline while preserving classical kinematic structure.
The learned inertial uncertainty is metrics-aware, with predicted covariance aligned to empirical error statistics in physical units.
Building on this preintegration model, an inertial odometry system~\cite{anonymous2025airio} addresses a complementary bottleneck, limited attitude observability under agile motion.
Feature analysis (PCA and t-SNE) shows that body-frame IMU representation is more expressive and compact than global-frame transformation because attitude couples linearly with gravitational acceleration in the body frame.
AirIO encodes attitude information with body-frame IMU features and fuses velocity predictions with AirIMU preintegration in an EKF where both modules carry learned uncertainty.
The system outperforms prior inertial odometry methods on UAV datasets without thrust commands or additional sensors, and it generalizes to unseen trajectories.
Together, these results deliver transferable inertial perception with corresponding uncertainty that remains reliable under high dynamics and complements visual covariance for multi-modal estimation.
With robust visual and inertial modules both carrying metrics-aware covariance, tuning-free cross-modal initialization and calibration become feasible across platforms.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Robust Visual-Inertial Initialization and Online Calibration.}
Traditional visual-inertial initializers often fail under exposure changes, low texture, or dynamic objects because fixed noise parameters cannot reflect measurement quality.
We developed a robust initialization and online calibration system~\cite{anonymous2026macinit} that uses uncertainty estimates from both vision and inertial modules to overcome this limitation.
The system derives visual pose covariance from learned feature-matching uncertainty and inertial covariance from learned preintegration, then weights the two sets of constraints according to their predicted reliability to produce a principled, stable initialization.
Because each constraint carries learned covariance, the optimizer naturally down-weights degraded measurements without per-scenario heuristics or hard outlier thresholds.
This removes per-platform parameter adjustment and enables tuning-free initialization across diverse environments and sensor configurations.
This stage converts modality-specific uncertainty estimates into a unified fusion interface for stable, tuning-free visual-inertial initialization and reliable downstream operation.
Once uncertainty is reliable for fusion, the next question is how to allocate computation safely on edge hardware.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Compute-Efficient Perception Deployment.}
After estimation and fusion are robust, uncertainty estimates guide where approximation is safe and where fidelity matters under edge compute limits.
First, we developed a full-stack quantization framework for learning-based visual odometry with learned covariance~\cite{anonymous2026quantmacvo} that combines a sim-to-real calibration strategy for zero-shot generalization with a kurtosis-guided hybrid scheme that applies expensive operations only where outliers dominate, yielding a $1.83\times$ speedup on embedded GPU hardware with negligible accuracy loss.
The framework quantizes both the feature extractor and the covariance head jointly, preserving uncertainty quality after compression.
Second, we introduced uncertainty-guided token merging for visual geometry transformers~\cite{anonymous2026come}, where a lightweight predictor ranks tokens by uncertainty and selectively merges uninformative ones, achieving up to $11.3\times$ speedup for dense 3D reconstruction and $7.2\times$ for semantic mapping on edge hardware while preserving quality in geometrically informative regions.
This stage turns uncertainty into a joint reliability and resource signal, enabling real-time deployment of robust perception systems without sacrificing critical spatial accuracy.

\section{Future Work}

Building on learned uncertainty for odometry and fusion, my future research extends the core idea into three directions, efficient deployment, semantic memory, and resilience in extreme environments.

\textbf{Efficient Robotic Spatial Perception.}
Spatial perception must run on the robots themselves, and our compression results show that learned models can be accelerated with minimal accuracy loss.
A deeper challenge, however, is to co-design uncertainty estimation with hardware-aware architectures so that the compute budget adapts online to predicted reliability and scene complexity.
I aim to build perception systems that allocate more computation to uncertain regions and less to confident ones, enabling always-on spatial perception on power-constrained platforms from handheld devices to aerial robots.
The expected outcome is real-time perception under strict power limits while preserving reliability in uncertain regions.

\textbf{Spatial Memory with Semantic Understanding.}
Current SLAM systems build geometric maps that often lack the semantics needed for long-horizon tasks such as object retrieval, task planning, and human-robot collaboration.
I plan to extend learned uncertainty into a spatial memory that jointly encodes geometry, semantics, and language-grounded embeddings, supporting queries such as ``where did I last see the red toolbox?'' and enabling robots to reason about scene changes over time.
By integrating vision-language representations with uncertainty-aware mapping, robots can plan and interact more reliably across repeated missions in evolving environments, using uncertainty to identify stale or unreliable map regions that need re-observation.
The expected outcome is long-horizon semantic retrieval and planning with explicit confidence in memory freshness.

\textbf{Robustness in Extreme Environments.}
Spatial perception research often targets urban and indoor settings, but high-impact applications lie in extreme and resource-limited domains such as subterranean rescue, deep-sea exploration, and planetary operations, where degraded GPS, limited communication, harsh lighting, and strict power budgets co-occur.
I aim to use learned uncertainty to make systems degrade gracefully by adapting fusion weights and compute allocation online.
When a sensor modality becomes unreliable, the system should automatically re-weight or disable it and maintain safe operation under severe distribution shift.
The expected outcome is safe autonomy under severe sensing degradation through uncertainty-driven sensor reweighting and compute reallocation.
Together, these directions advance spatial perception that is accurate, deployable, and resilient for real-world robots.
